Cap Rate — What the property earns on its own
Cap Rate = Net Operating Income ÷ Purchase Price. Net Operating Income is your rent income after vacancy minus all operating expenses (tax, insurance, maintenance, utilities, management) — but not the mortgage. This makes it financing-neutral, so you can compare any two properties regardless of how they're purchased.
Why it matters: Higher cap rate = more income per dollar of property value. It tells you the "yield" of the asset itself.
Minneapolis duplex benchmarks (2024–2025):
● 5–7%+ — Healthy yield for Minneapolis; strong cash flow potential
● 4–5% — Fair; common in high-demand neighborhoods (Uptown, North Loop, Longfellow)
● Below 4% — Thin yield; you're buying primarily for appreciation, not income
Cash-on-Cash Return — Your actual return on dollars invested
Cash-on-Cash = Annual Net Cash Flow ÷ Total Cash Invested. Total cash invested includes your down payment, closing costs, and any upfront repairs. Net cash flow is what remains after all monthly costs including the mortgage.
Why it matters: This is the most honest measure of how your actual dollars are performing right now. Negative means the property costs you money each month; positive means it pays you. As an owner-occupant, you can think of it relative to what you'd otherwise spend on rent.
Minneapolis owner-occupant benchmarks:
● 5–8%+ — Strong; property is generating meaningful income beyond your costs
● 0–5% — Acceptable; you're near break-even, building equity while living affordably
● Negative — Cash-flow negative; common with 2024–2025 rates. You're subsidizing the property monthly, but may still build significant wealth through equity and appreciation
Gross Rent Multiplier — A quick value screen
GRM = Purchase Price ÷ Annual Gross Rent. It tells you how many years of full rent it would take to pay for the property. It ignores expenses and financing, so it's less precise than cap rate — but it's a fast way to filter properties before doing deep math.
Why it matters: Lower GRM = better value relative to rental income. A GRM of 12 means the property costs 12× its annual rent; 20 means it costs 20×. Useful for quickly comparing multiple listings.
Minneapolis duplex benchmarks (2024–2025):
● Below 12 — Strong value; rent income is high relative to price
● 12–16 — Fair; typical for Minneapolis duplexes in most neighborhoods
● Above 16 — Expensive relative to income; common in highly appreciating areas or during high-price periods
DSCR — Can the rental income cover the mortgage?
DSCR = Net Operating Income ÷ Annual Debt Service (mortgage payments). Think of it as: "for every $1 of mortgage payment, how many dollars does the rental income generate?" A DSCR of 1.25 means the property earns $1.25 in net operating income for every $1 it owes on the mortgage.
Why it matters: This is the number lenders use to decide if an investment property can support its own loan. Even as an owner-occupant, a strong DSCR means your tenant is essentially covering your mortgage — you're just living for free. A DSCR below 1.0 means the rental income alone can't cover the mortgage; you're subsidizing the shortfall from your own pocket.
Benchmarks:
● 1.25 or higher — Strong; rental income more than covers the debt. Most lenders require this for investment loans
● 1.0–1.24 — Marginal; rental income covers the debt but with little cushion. Vacancy or a repair can flip it negative
● Below 1.0 — Property can't service its own debt from rent alone. Common in high-rate environments for owner-occupants who are offsetting with their own income
IRR — Your true annualized return on this investment
IRR (Internal Rate of Return) is the annualized return that makes the total value of all your cash flows — money in, money out, and the eventual sale proceeds — equal to zero. In plain terms: if you put cash in today, receive or pay monthly over 10 years, and then sell, what single annual interest rate would explain all of that? That's your IRR.
Why it matters: IRR is the most complete measure of investment performance because it accounts for the timing of every dollar. A property with great cash flow but low appreciation might have a lower IRR than one with modest cash flow but strong equity growth. It lets you compare real estate against other investments (stocks, bonds, paying off debt) on equal footing — using a single percentage.
Minneapolis duplex benchmarks (10-year horizon):
● 12%+ — Excellent; outpacing most stock market averages on a risk-adjusted basis
● 8–12% — Solid; competitive with equity market long-term averages
● Below 8% — Thin; consider whether the illiquidity and management burden is worth it vs. simpler investments

What is depreciation? The IRS lets rental property owners deduct a portion of the building's value each year as a "wear and tear" expense — even though the building isn't actually losing value (and may be gaining it). This deduction reduces your taxable rental income on paper, which means you can owe less in taxes on money you actually received.

Why is it valuable? It's essentially a government subsidy for owning rental property. If your rental unit generates $12,000/year in rent and you have an $8,000 depreciation deduction, you only pay income tax on $4,000 — despite having $12,000 in your bank account. At a 22% bracket, that's roughly $1,760/year you keep instead of sending to the IRS. Over 10 years, that's $17,600+ in tax savings from depreciation alone.

The catch: When you sell, the IRS "recaptures" the depreciation you claimed and taxes it at up to 25% — separate from your capital gains rate. This is called depreciation recapture. It reduces (but rarely eliminates) the benefit. A 1031 exchange can defer both taxes indefinitely if you roll proceeds into another investment property.

Calculation: (Purchase Price × 80% building share × 50% rental unit share) ÷ 27.5 years. Land value is excluded — it's not depreciable. Consult a CPA to optimize Schedule E and confirm your specific numbers.

🏠 Minneapolis & Minnesota Homebuyer Programs & Local Rules ▼
🏙️ City — Minneapolis Down payment assistance · rental rules · TISH ▼
🏙️
Minneapolis Homes Access DPA
Up to $20,000 for Minneapolis primary residences
▼

The Minneapolis Homes: Access program offers up to $20,000 in down payment assistance for buyers purchasing a primary residence within Minneapolis city limits.

Income limits and application requirements apply. Contact Minneapolis Community Planning and Economic Development or visit minneapolismn.gov — Homebuyer Resources ↗ for current details and to confirm duplex eligibility.

📋
Rental License Exemption
Owner-occupants generally exempt from licensing
▼

Minneapolis requires a rental license for landlords who don't live on the property. If you occupy one unit as your primary residence, you are generally exempt — a meaningful operational simplification.

If you ever vacate and rent both units, a license is required (~$450 at ownership change + annual renewal by March 1). New 2025 rules: mandatory disclosures before lease signing and 30-day pre-eviction notice. See minneapolismn.gov — Rental Licensing ↗.

📈
Rent Stabilization Status
No rent control currently in effect (as of 2026)
▼

Minneapolis voters approved a charter amendment in 2021 giving the City Council authority to regulate rents, but no ordinance has been enacted as of early 2026. A proposal capping annual increases at ~3% has been discussed but not passed.

Note: St. Paul has a separate 3% annual cap — Minneapolis does not. You can currently raise rent at lease renewal. Monitor minneapolismn.gov/residents/housing ↗ for updates.

📑
TISH — Truth in Sale of Housing
Required Minneapolis disclosure report at closing
▼

Minneapolis requires a Truth in Sale of Housing (TISH) disclosure report for all one- and two-family residential properties sold within city limits. The seller pays for a licensed evaluator to inspect the property and document code deficiencies.

TISH is not a full home inspection but highlights known defects. Cost to seller: typically $200–$400. As a buyer, you should still get your own independent inspection. Required before listing — must be available at showings. See minneapolismn.gov/residents/housing/tish ↗.

🏛️ County — Hennepin Homestead · HERO energy loans · Fix-Up Fund · sustainability ▼
🏡
Homestead Exemption
Lower tax rate for owner-occupied duplex units
▼

When you occupy one unit of a duplex, the owner-occupied portion qualifies for the 1.00% homestead class rate (first $500K of value). The rental unit is assessed at the higher 1.25% non-homestead rate.

This split can save $200–$400+ per year. File a Homestead Application with Hennepin County Assessor by December 31 to receive the benefit the following tax year. Call 612-348-3046 for help or visit hennepin.us — Homestead ↗.

⚡
HERO — Home Energy Rebates & Loans
Low-interest financing for energy upgrades
▼

Hennepin County's Home Energy Rebates Online (HERO) program partners with CEE (Center for Energy and Environment) to offer below-market loans for energy efficiency upgrades — insulation, windows, furnace/boiler replacement, heat pumps, and more.

Loan amounts up to $35,000 at rates as low as 1.99% (income-qualified). Multi-family owner-occupied properties (including duplexes) are eligible. Some upgrades also qualify for federal IRA tax credits (25C/25D) on top of county financing.

Apply through CEE: mncee.org/home-energy-loan ↗ · Hennepin County Energy info: hennepin.us/residents/energy-environment ↗

🔧
Hennepin County Fix-Up Fund
Rehab loans for home repair & accessibility
▼

The Fix-Up Fund (administered by MN Housing/CEE) provides fixed-rate home improvement loans to moderate-income homeowners in Hennepin County. Eligible projects include roof/furnace replacement, electrical and plumbing upgrades, and accessibility modifications.

Loan amounts: Up to $50,000 · Rates below market (varies by income) · Owner-occupied 1–4 unit properties eligible, including duplexes. Income limits apply. Contact CEE at 612-335-5858 or see mncee.org/fix-up-fund ↗.

🏠
Property Tax Deferral & Tax Portal
Senior deferral · look up proposed tax by parcel
▼

Hennepin County offers a Senior Citizen Property Tax Deferral program allowing homeowners 65+ with income under $96,000 to defer a portion of property taxes (at 3% annual interest). While primarily for retirees, it's worth knowing if buying an owner-occupied duplex long-term.

Effective rates: Hennepin County effective property tax rate runs ~1.17–1.19% of market value annually. Owner-occupied duplexes benefit from split homestead classification. Check your proposed tax at Hennepin County Property Tax Portal ↗ using the parcel number.

Senior deferral: hennepin.us/residents/property/senior-property-tax-deferral ↗

🌿
Sustainability & Energy Rebates
EV charger, solar, weatherization, heat pumps
▼

Hennepin County's sustainability programs include rebates and technical assistance for:

  • Solar installations — Xcel Energy Solar Rewards + federal 30% ITC
  • EV charging stations — Xcel Energy Make Ready program (up to $500/charger)
  • Weatherization — free or low-cost services for income-qualified households
  • Heat pump water heaters — $400–$750 rebates (Xcel/CenterPoint)

See hennepin.us energy programs ↗ and xcelenergy.com/savings_and_rebates ↗.

🌾 State — Minnesota MN Housing DPA loans · deed tax · MRT ▼
💰
MN Housing Start Up Loan
Up to $18,000 — deferred, 0% interest DPA
▼

Minnesota Housing's Start Up program offers a deferred second mortgage of up to $18,000 for down payment and closing costs. The loan is interest-free and repayment is deferred until sale, refinance, or the home is no longer a primary residence.

Limits: Max purchase price $310,000 in the Twin Cities metro · Minimum credit score 640 · Income limits apply · Requires a homebuyer education course. Apply through an approved lender — see mnhousing.gov — Start Up ↗.

⚠ Verify before applying: Purchase price and income limits are updated annually. Confirm current figures at mnhousing.gov before making an offer.

🌱
First-Generation Homebuyer Loan
Up to $35,000 — partially forgivable over 20 years
▼

MN Housing's First Generation Homebuyer Loan provides up to $35,000 for buyers whose parents never owned a home (or lost one to foreclosure). Must be paired with a Start Up first mortgage.

Forgiveness: 50% forgiven after 10 years of owner-occupancy; remaining 50% after 20 years. Deferred, 0% interest. Limited slots — first-come, first-served. See mnhousing.gov — First Generation Homebuyer Loan ↗.

⚠ Verify before applying: This program is funded annually and periodically pauses mid-year when funds run out. Apply early in the calendar year for the best chance of securing funds.

📊
Minnesota Deed Tax & MRT
State closing cost taxes to budget for
▼

Two MN-specific closing costs to budget for:

  • Mortgage Registry Tax (MRT): 0.23% of loan amount — paid by the buyer. On a $240,000 loan = $552.
  • State Deed Tax: 0.33% of sale price — paid by the seller at closing. On a $300,000 sale = $990.

MRT is included in this calculator's closing cost estimate. Deed Tax is reflected in the Sell Card's selling cost breakdown. See revenue.state.mn.us — MRT & Deed Tax ↗.

🇺🇸 Federal FHA loans · mortgage interest deduction · IRA energy credits ▼
🏦
FHA Loan — 3.5% Down Option
Lower barrier to entry with mortgage insurance tradeoff
▼

FHA loans (Federal Housing Administration) allow as little as 3.5% down with a credit score of 580+. Owner-occupied duplexes are eligible — you can use the rental unit's expected income to help qualify.

Trade-offs: Requires both an upfront MIP (1.75% of loan, rolled in or paid at closing) and annual MIP (~0.55–0.85%/yr) for the life of the loan if you put less than 10% down. Conventional PMI cancels at 20% equity; FHA MIP does not.

2025 FHA loan limits (Hennepin County): $524,225 for 1-unit · $671,200 for 2-unit (duplex). See hud.gov — FHA Loan Limits ↗.

💸
Mortgage Interest Deduction
Deduct mortgage interest if you itemize
▼

If you itemize federal deductions, the interest portion of your mortgage payment may be deductible. For an owner-occupied duplex, you can deduct the interest attributable to your unit; the rental unit's share is a business expense on Schedule E.

Standard deduction for 2025: $15,000 (single) / $30,000 (married filing jointly). You only benefit from itemizing if total deductions exceed this. Many first-time buyers do not itemize. The monthly breakdown card above estimates your potential tax savings. Consult a CPA for your specific situation.

⚡
IRA Energy Tax Credits (25C & 25D)
Up to 30% federal credit for energy upgrades
▼

The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) created substantial federal tax credits for energy-efficient home improvements:

  • §25C Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit: 30% of cost, up to $3,200/yr for insulation, heat pumps, windows, doors, electrical panels
  • §25D Residential Clean Energy Credit: 30% of cost (no annual cap) for solar panels, battery storage, geothermal heat pumps

Credits apply to your primary residence only (not the rental unit). Stackable with Xcel/CenterPoint utility rebates and Hennepin County loans. See IRS.gov — Energy Credits ↗.

🏠
Capital Gains Exclusion (§121)
Exclude up to $250K / $500K of profit at sale
▼

If you've owned and lived in the home as your primary residence for at least 2 of the last 5 years before selling, you can exclude up to $250,000 of capital gain (single) or $500,000 (married filing jointly) from federal tax.

For an owner-occupied duplex, the exclusion applies to your owner-occupied portion. The rental unit's gain is generally taxable (depreciation recapture at 25% + capital gains rate). This is a major tax benefit of the owner-occupant strategy. Consult a CPA before selling. See IRS Topic 701 — Home Sale Exclusion ↗.

Program details, income limits, and rates change frequently. Verify current eligibility at mnhousing.gov ↗, minneapolismn.gov ↗, and hennepin.us ↗ before applying. This calculator is for educational purposes only and does not constitute financial, tax, or legal advice.
